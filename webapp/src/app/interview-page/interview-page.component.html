Interview about Literary Activities and Experiences in Canada During the COVID-19 Pandemic Period
Organization: Metatron Press
Date: 7 February 2022
Interviewee: Ashley, Executive Director, Quebec Writers Federation
Interview conducted with Jason Camlot by email
1. Did you have to cancel any live events due to the pandemic restrictions (beginning in March 2020)?  Can you give an example?

We were not releasing books that Spring, so nothing was explicitly canceled. We did, however, have to withhold from organizing book launches in the Fall of 2020. The fate of our Spring launch this year remains uncertain. It’s been destabilizing not knowing what to plan for (in-person, digital).

2. questionDo you recall what happened when you decided to move events online?  How did this happen? What were you feeling? What solutions did you come up with?  What made you decide to move events online? How did the first event go?

The first event we held online was unrelated to our publicity efforts. When Diane Di Prima passed away, I felt immense grief and gratitude for the work Di Prima wrote in her lifetime. I decided to organize a marathon reading of her Revolutionary Letters online. I reached out to the many writers I am connected to across the world and organized a complete reading of the text over Google Hangouts that featured 27 voices.

This reading was fueled by what I sensed was a public need to come together, outside of the marketing and promotion realm of publishing, to celebrate an incredible woman and poet who dared to dream of a new world. And it did feel very special, albeit it being online. To connect with one another~ via technology~ to partake in creating something together in real-time through such a powerful text… It was truly a special event.

What I will add, as well, is that I also function as a poet in the world. The readings I have been invited to partake in throughout the pandemic made me feel particularly existential. Online readings seem to lack the very things I cherish most about public expressions of creative work: haptic feedback, socializing, and the sense that a group of people are literally holding physical space for your work.

3. How have your online events developed between March 2020 and now?

I’ve been hesitant to organize online events. Even though Metatron is viewed as being a digi-savvy press, it just hasn’t felt right to me. It lacks a depth that I crave from public readings.

 

While I do appreciate and see the value in online readings, the reason I came to organizing events was purely real-world community. When I started organizing events in 2012, it’s because I sensed there was a need in Montreal for literary events that defied convention and united people, making space for new voices to be featured. I wanted to see poets read in weird spaces, to diverse audiences. And so it felt, in many ways, that literary events online were only drawing in specifically literary audiences—which is something I’ve worked very hard to defy. I really want to make poetry accessible to all and expand who we interpret as being a poetry audience, and the online literary reading just feels too acutely literary for me, if that makes sense. It is too boxed in, literally and figuratively.

4. Can you share a story about an online reading or event that seemed particularly important or powerful to you during this pandemic period?  What made it special?

The Diane Di Prima reading was really quite spectacular. The sheer scale of it, and the many technical issues that could have arisen did not, and it felt like some sort of digital miracle that it all worked out the way it did.

Working with Anteism was also a great experience. We held a private reading with only writers and producers present. We managed to feature 12 readers. And working with Anteism on production was an amazing experience. I felt very supported by them, and think that the end result looks and sounds great. I think, for me, there is a difference between people reading from their laptop cameras and speakers, and then having access to higher-level production. For online events, I definitely prefer having a bit of a budget to make it look and sound great, and give readers a space outside of their intimate homes to share their work. I am very happy with how that series turned out, and hope to feature more writers in that capacity again.

Link to readings:  https://youtu.be/PC-v7XQe4AU

My writing community here in Montreal often utilizes casual readings to test out new writing and to even inspire the writing of new work. So, it felt particularly potent for me to be able to organize something like that in the middle of the pandemic. I sensed a deeper sense of gratitude amongst us all for the kind of power a live literary reading holds for individuals and communities. Many of us had not read in so long and were perhaps a bit out of practice, but the vulnerability and honesty of that reality was incredibly moving and emotional. Many of us shared tears together in that space.

5. How would you describe the atmosphere and effect of the online events you have organized?  What role have they played in sharing the work of authors, in maintaining community, in creating new extended connections, in giving people respite in a stressful time, etc.?

Our online events have been less focused on marketing and publicity and more on nurturing community and creating visibility around writers in the Montreal community, whether they are Metatron authors or not. That is actually the function of Metatron readings~ Not necessarily to promote the work we publish, but to support up-and-coming writers in the community.  I am not quite sure what effect our events have had, though. That’s the thing with digital readings: aside from the chat, there is so little feedback that happens after the event. I’m not so sure they resonate as deeply as a real-world event might.

6. How do you feel the pandemic events have changed literary activity in your community or in a wider Canadian or global context?

I think it’s forced people to adapt their work online, which I don’t think is negative, but I don’t think this format is for everyone. It does, however, give people all over the world access to experiencing the work of local poets—which is fantastic.

 

Writers and publishers were lucky in the sense that many artists were not able to adapt their work so easily online. While online readings are imperfect and (in my opinion) awkward, they also shed light on the potential of technology to help writers and publishers reach new audiences. While I haven’t quite seized this specific moment to do so, I do see the value in integrating digital readings with live readings in the future.

7. What impact do you think these events will have on the future?

For me, I would love for all of our future in-person events to also be available online. I love how much more inclusive online readings can be, not just for people who may live in other parts of the world but also for accessibility reasons.

 

I think we will continue to see festivals and reading series shift their efforts online, especially given that many of the available grants are favoring these activities. Additionally, it is cheaper to host events online, which is a benefit I believe publishers will take advantage of.

9. How were your events or activities organized prior to pandemic restrictions?  Where did they take place?  Can you give a brief characterization of the events or scene that your events created?

It was 2012 when we organized our first reading, two years before we launched our publishing program. We held it in a friend's rustic loft in the quaint Mile Ex neighborhood in Montréal. Since then we have organized over 50 readings in Canada and the United States, featuring more than 300 writers. We have done readings online and outdoors in fields, in new and used book stores and historical buildings, art galleries and music venues, creative hubs and bars, greenhouses and many off-the-beaten-path spaces. Our ethos around readings is to create unique and irreplaceable literary events that people will feel lucky they were present for.

 

Typically, Metatron events are all about atmosphere. I always go out of my way to do special lighting design and put a lot of work into conceptualizing the spatial design. I aim for our events to be experiences, readings that can’t be reproduced. That is something I’m not able to add to online events, and it’s why I look forward to it being safe enough to gather in person again!

 

With that said, Anteism was great to work with because they made our videos unique by integrating live graphics, moving text and music.

10. Please feel free to add anything else, any other thoughts, feelings, information that the questions have not covered.

There was one event I organized in collaboration with the Blue Met Festival that caused me to have a bit of panic attack. To be fair—my anxiety was at an all-time high at this time. We were filming footage with Anteism to edit together for an event based on translation. As opposed to the first Anteism reading—which was live—this was more of a production, in the sense that we were going to then edit the footage together to create a cohesive video. I, all-of-a-sudden, felt like my role as a community organizer had shifted to being some sort of television personality. I could see myself talking on the screen right in front of me during my introductions, and it was distracting me from being present with the whole thing. I had an extremely difficult time acting ‘natural’ because I was exceptionally worried about how I looked, whether or not the camera was picking up the blemishes on my face. It made it difficult to be present. And all the stop and go of filming interrupted the “flow” of the event in a way that wouldn’t have happened had it been live on stage. It was just so awkward, and all the writers seemed so uncomfortable, and it really just felt wrong in so many ways. 

That was, in my opinion, the worst event I have organized (although it did turn out looking ok!), and I haven’t organized an online event since.

I think that publishers and writers have unfortunately had to adapt to a world they didn’t intentionally consent to. While it is great that we have the tool of online readings available to us, I’m not exactly sure many of us (readers included) are as inspired by online events as one would think.

~

One more thing, Metatron has utilized online readings in the past before the pandemic. On three occasions we have broadcast digital readings to a live audience from individuals featured at the reading who were not physically present in Montreal. We projected the (live) readings like a film would be. I love digital readings for this reason, to amplify the experience of in-person readings.
